Adaptive wireless receivers rely on automatic modulation classification (AMC) to select the correct demodulator for incoming signals. We demonstrate that adversarial perturbations targeting the AMC classifier constitute a control-plane denial-of-service attack: by causing the receiver to select the wrong demodulator, the attacker collapses data-link integrity without corrupting the physical waveform itself. Using Carlini-Wagner L2 attacks against an Adaptive Wavelet Network (AWN) classifier on the RML2016.10a dataset, we show that CRC pass rates drop from near-100% to 6% under adversarial AMC-driven demodulation (Adv+AMC), while the same perturbed signals maintain 94% CRC when demodulated with the correct (oracle) modulation type. This 88-percentage-point gap between Adv+Oracle and Adv+AMC confirms that the attack operates at the control plane, not the data plane. We further show that achieving equivalent link disruption via conventional wideband jamming requires orders-of-magnitude more power, establishing adversarial AMC attacks as an efficient, stealthy threat to software-defined radio receivers.
